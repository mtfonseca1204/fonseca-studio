WEB3 · INSTITUTIONAL PRODUCT DESIGN

Transparent.space

Transparent.space is a Web3 platform focused on verifiable proof of Market Maker performance, bringing together critical data such as SLA, liquidity, spreads, uptime, pool depth, and historical behavior in a single institutional environment.

Problem

Institutions managing millions in crypto liquidity had no reliable way to check if their Market Makers (the firms that keep markets liquid) were actually performing. When a service agreement was breached, teams took 15 to 20 minutes to find the problem, jumping between spreadsheets and disconnected dashboards.

Solution

We designed a single-screen platform where SLA status, liquidity trends, and historical performance are visible at a glance, with full detail one click away. Status first → data second → drill-down on demand.

Result

~30% faster to find answers in testing. Task completion went from 61→88%. The product opened early partnership conversations with Kraken and Worldchain.

MY ROLE

Founding Product Designer: building the product from zero

I joined Transparent.space before there was a product. No screens, no design system, no shared language between business and engineering. Just a hard problem and a small team trying to solve it.

As Founding Product Designer, I wasn't handed a brief and told to make it look good. I helped define what the product should be: what users see first, what data matters most, and how complex financial information becomes something a desk operator reads in seconds and an analyst trusts down to the number.

Product direction: Shaped IA, user flows, and core UX decisions alongside the PM and engineering lead, from first wireframe to validated v2.

Design system: Built 120+ modular components in Figma so design and dev shared one language from day one.

Validation loops: Ran usability sessions, cut what didn't work, and measured impact: 61% to 88% task completion between v1 and v2.

Visual language: Defined the blueprint-inspired system that signals precision and trust before anyone reads a data point.

Founding design means owning the full arc: problem, structure, interface, system, and proof that it works.

THE PROBLEM

15 to 20 minutes to find a problem that costs money every second.

Picture this: a service agreement gets breached. The operations team opens Dune, pulls a spreadsheet, checks a manual report. Fifteen to twenty minutes later, they finally know what went wrong. In fast-moving markets, every minute has a price.

And it wasn't just about emergencies. Day to day, institutions had no standard way to compare Market Makers side by side. The data lived everywhere, was often self-reported, and was hard to verify.

The real enemy wasn't missing data. It was data in the wrong places, in the wrong format, at the wrong time.

ACTUAL SCENARIO

Market Makers keep crypto moving. Nobody could prove they were doing their job.

Market Makers are the firms that provide liquidity to crypto markets: they keep spreads tight, pools deep, and exchanges running. Institutions depend on them completely. But measuring whether they're actually delivering? That was another story.

Performance evaluation today is:

  • Fragmented (Dune, DeFiLlama, proprietary dashboards, manual reports)
  • Poorly standardized
  • Difficult to audit and compare

What users told us hurt most:

No single view of performance across time and metrics

No real proof that SLA agreements were being met

Built for analysts, not for the people making decisions

Numbers without context, hard to read, harder to act on

That's the gap Transparent.space was built to close. Keep scrolling to see how.

THE SOLUTION

One screen. One source of truth. Under 2 minutes.

One screen. See the full picture. Drill down only when you need to. We designed so a manager gets the answer in seconds, and an analyst gets the depth without losing their place.

01

Information Architecture: answer the big question first

We started with one question: "Is this Market Maker healthy right now?" Everything else is secondary. We mapped two journeys (quick executive scan and deep analyst review) onto the same screen. Four data layers: SLA Status, Liquidity Behavior, Market Comparison, and Methodology.

02

Component Design: 120+ pieces, one system

Built a modular design system of 120+ components in Figma. Same card works as a quick summary or a detailed view. Light mode by default: clean, serious, built for high-stakes decisions.

03

Interaction Design: go deeper without getting lost

Every screen has a simple summary view and an expandable detail view. Breadcrumbs show where you are. Alerts are clear: agreement fulfilled or not fulfilled, with trend data available on expand.

04

Validation: cut the noise, keep what matters

v1 showed 12 metrics at once. Two operators told us: "I don't know where to start." We cut to 4 critical metrics on the main view and moved the rest behind drill-down. In v2 with 4 operators, task completion went from 61% to 88%. Finding the status got 40% faster.

The goal: find an SLA problem in under 2 minutes. Legacy tools took 15 to 20.

Four stages. One screen. Here's exactly how we built it.

KEY DECISIONS

Three choices that changed everything

SLA: yes or no, not maybe

98.7% uptime sounds fine. But did the Market Maker meet the agreement or not? We replaced confusing percentages with a clear answer: fulfilled or not fulfilled. Executives get it in seconds. Analysts still get the full breakdown one level down.

Show the trend, not just the number

A $200K pool depth means nothing if you don't know where it's heading. We made direction the hero: every metric shows whether it's rising, falling, or stable. A number in motion tells a story a static number never will.

4 metrics, not 12

More data isn't better data. v1 overwhelmed users. Two operators said it plainly: "I don't know where to start." We kept 4 metrics up front and hid the rest behind drill-down. Status identification got 40% faster.

DISCOVERY & RESEARCH

We studied the best, then built something better

We couldn't run traditional user research at scale. Instead, we studied how the best tools in finance and crypto organize complex data, and what makes institutional users trust a screen in under 10 seconds.

Crypto data platforms

  • Studied: Dune Analytics, DeFiLlama. The tools teams already used, and where they fell short.
  • Learned: too much data, badly organized, spread across too many places

Institutional financial tools

  • Studied: Bloomberg Terminal. The benchmark for density, trust, and professional tone.
  • Learned: executives decide in 10 seconds; analysts need depth without a separate product

Performance & SLA dashboards

  • Studied: Grafana, Datadog. How monitoring tools structure alerts and status.
  • Learned: charts should guide the eye, not just display numbers

Users don't need more data. They need the right data, in the right order, with the right level of trust. In institutional products, looking serious is part of the product.

Users, stakeholders & product objectives

Primary users:

Institutional Clients: crypto funds, DeFi protocols, exchanges, Web3 treasuries. Need quick comparison, verifiable data, and an executive analysis layer.

Market Makers: trading desks needing to demonstrate SLA compliance. Need self-assessment tools and proof of performance against agreed SLAs.

Product objectives:

  • Create a single layer of truth: a data operating system for Web3 liquidity
  • Enable comparison without gamification: neutral side-by-side, no leaderboard bias
  • Single-screen mental model: understand health at a glance, dive deeper without changing context
DESIGN CONCEPT

Designed to feel like infrastructure, not a startup dashboard

Before anyone reads a number, the product has to feel credible. We built a blueprint-inspired visual system: precise grids, clear structure, nothing decorative. It reads like engineering documentation, not a crypto landing page.

Intentional Simplicity

Every pixel earns its place. The main screen answers one question: is this Market Maker healthy? Everything else waits behind a click.

Strict Hierarchy

Status before numbers. Yes/no before percentages. Trend before absolute value. The layout tells your eye where to go.

Visual Neutrality

No rankings. No badges. No colors pushing you toward a "winner." Same metrics, same layout, neutral comparison. The data speaks.

Radical Transparency

Grids and quadrants make everything visible. Nothing hidden behind decoration. If it matters, you can see it and compare it.

WHAT I LEARNED

Lessons Learned

In institutional products, looking polished can actually hurt trust. Every time we added something "to make it feel better," users trusted it less. Restraint wasn't a limitation. It was the strategy.

Full lessons from the project

UI simplification without system simplification doesn't work.

In v1, we tried to reduce complexity by simplifying the visual layer while keeping the underlying IA unchanged. Usability sessions showed the same confusion patterns. The complexity had just shifted from visual to conceptual. The real improvement came when we redesigned the IA, not just the UI.

Designing for two user types requires explicit prioritization.

The tension between the executive user (scan in 10 seconds) and the analyst user (full data depth) almost derailed the IA multiple times. The resolution: the product always loads in executive mode, with analyst depth accessible but never forced.

Comparison without gamification is harder, and more honest.

Leaderboard-style ranking was discarded after recognizing gamification creates visual bias. Side-by-side comparison with neutral UI forces users to evaluate nuance, not chase rankings.

AI-assisted workflows change the speed of ideation, not the quality of decisions.

AI tools cut our ideation phase from weeks to days (~60% faster cycles). But what to prioritize, what to cut, and what the user actually needs still required the same depth of research and reasoning.
