MY ROLE

Product Designer: 15,000+ sign-ups from a 40K+ organic community

Hedgehog had no product to show. No screenshots, no demo, no user base. My job was to design a landing page that converted a 40K+ organic community across X (14.6K), Telegram (14K), Discord, and Web3 networks into product waitlist sign-ups, without a single dollar in paid traffic.

As the sole Product Designer, I owned the full conversion architecture: research, page structure, copy direction, visual language, and the reasoning behind every section. This wasn't a marketing page with a signup form. It was the first product experience, and the first proof of market fit: 15,000+ users joined the waitlist at 37.5% conversion from an audience that benchmarks at 2 to 8%.

Conversion architecture: Designed the four-stage funnel: value, trust, mechanics, conversion. Each section earned the right to ask for the email.

Organic community conversion: Turned X, Telegram, Discord, and Web3 network traffic into 15,000+ product waitlist sign-ups at 37.5%, against a 2 to 8% industry benchmark, with $0 paid acquisition across a 40K+ social footprint.

Visual & copy direction: Institutional tone, no hype, no unfinished UI mockups. Concept visuals and strong copy outperformed placeholder screens.

Product loop: Used landing page behavior data to shape the product's IA, onboarding copy, and first usability cohort from waitlist sign-ups.

When there's nothing to show, design has to do the selling. Trust becomes the product.

THE PROBLEM

How do you convert skeptics on a product they can't see?

The audience is institutional and professional crypto users. They bounce at anything that feels premature, salesy, or unfinished.

Before they share an email, they need to believe the product is real and that the team actually understands the problem.

Most landing pages ask too early. We flipped it: earn trust first, convert last.

ACTUAL SCENARIO

No product. No proof. Still need early adopters.

Hedgehog was pre-launch. No screenshots, no live demo, no testimonials, no track record. All we had was a strong concept, a credible team, and a problem worth solving.

The usual playbook (feature lists, product shots, social proof) wasn't available. The page had to make people believe in what the product would become, using only words, structure, and tone.

The conversion context:

Audience: institutional users and experienced DeFi participants. Skeptical by default.

Stage: pre-launch. No live product, no user base.

Channel: organic only. X (14.6K), Telegram (14K), Discord, and Web3 dev networks. 40K+ combined social reach. Zero paid traffic.

Goal: waitlist sign-up as the single measurable action.

Validation: test positioning and messaging before the full build.

That's the starting point. Here's how we designed the page to convert anyway.

THE SOLUTION

Four stages. Trust first, conversion last.

01

Hero: frame the value, don't oversell

Clear proposition, institutional tone, no hype. CTA present but not forced. No UI mockups: unfinished screens signal "not ready" to this audience. We used concept visuals instead.

02

Trust layer: earn credibility

Problem framing that matches the user's reality. On-chain focus signals technical depth. Sections on SLA, governance, and system integrity: the questions institutional users ask that consumer products ignore.

03

Mechanics: make it believable

3-step explanation: what it is, how it works, why it's different. Accurate but not overwhelming. Enough to believe, not enough to audit. Full docs linked for those who want depth.

Users who read this section converted at significantly higher rates. This is the trust inflection point.

04

Conversion: close at peak intent

Repeated CTAs with evolving microcopy: awareness → intent → commitment. Email only: every extra field dropped conversion. Segmentation moved to onboarding.

Four stages. One funnel. Trust first, email last.

KEY DECISIONS

Three choices that drove 37.5% conversion

No UI mockups

Placeholder screens signal "not ready." Concept visuals plus strong copy outperformed UI mockups. Users who saw no product stayed longer and converted more.

Email only in the form

Each extra field dropped conversion. Segmentation happens in onboarding, where context is better and response rates are higher.

The page as a research tool

Behavioral data (which sections held attention, where users dropped) shaped the product's IA and onboarding copy. The landing page was the first usability test of the value proposition.

RESEARCH & BENCHMARKING

We studied what makes institutional users trust a page

Before designing a single section, I analyzed how comparable products in institutional and Web3 contexts build trust and drive conversion before launch.

Institutional SaaS / FinTech launches

  • Studied: Brex, Ramp, Mercury, Stripe (early launch pages)
  • Learned: credibility first (team, backers, problem framing) before any product or CTA
  • Applied: Hedgehog earned trust before asking for the email

Web3 / DeFi product launches

  • Studied: Polymarket, Aave, Uniswap v3, Blur
  • Learned: on-chain credibility matters more than traditional social proof
  • Applied: copy addressed mechanics, governance, and system integrity

Waitlist-driven funnels

  • Studied: Linear, Superhuman, Notion, Phantom wallet
  • Learned: users need to understand the benefit before they value exclusivity
  • Applied: waitlist CTA framed around early access, not just "sign up"

Key insight driving the architecture:

The funnel is: Understand → Believe → Trust → Act. Most pages skip to Act. This one earned each step first.

VISUAL DESIGN DECISIONS

Designed to feel like a tool, not a crypto hype page

Every visual choice served the conversion strategy. Nothing decorative. Nothing that eroded trust.

Institutional typography for clarity and authority

Serious typography, no playful fonts, no crypto gradients. Institutional users judge credibility in one second. The type had to pass that test.

Restrained color palette

Dark backgrounds, high-contrast highlights. Feels like a financial tool, not a consumer app. Color reserved for CTAs only.

Information hierarchy with clear section anchors

One dominant message per section. Clear path from heading to copy to CTA. Built for F-pattern scanning.

Spacing as a trust signal

Generous whitespace signals confidence. Dense layouts signal rush. Restraint was deliberate, not lazy.

Conversion performance & validation signals

The page was distributed through a 40K+ organic community: X (14.6K followers), Telegram (14K members), Discord, and Web3 developer networks. No paid acquisition at any stage.

Conversion performance:

Total waitlist sign-ups: 15,000+ users converted to the product waitlist from organic community traffic alone.

Organic reach: 40K+ combined social footprint across X (14.6K), Telegram (14K), Discord, and Web3 networks.

Product waitlist conversion: 37.5% of organic community visitors joined. B2B/Web3 benchmark is 2-8% for cold traffic. At 37.5%, the landing outperformed industry norms by roughly 5×.

Acquisition cost: $0 paid traffic. All sign-ups came from X, Telegram, Discord, and Web3 dev networks.

CTA engagement: consistent interaction across hero (above-the-fold) and footer CTAs, confirming the two-CTA architecture was effective for both fast-deciders and thorough readers.

Scroll depth: high retention through the trust and mechanics sections. Users weren't bouncing at the explanation layer, validating the decision to include system transparency.

Product validation signals:

Engagement patterns indicated qualified interest, not casual curiosity. Users who signed up had spent meaningful time on the page before converting.

The 15,000+ waitlist became the acquisition layer for the product beta. Users who signed up were segmented by engagement level and onboarded in priority order.

Conversion data directly informed product communication decisions. The sections with highest engagement shaped the onboarding copy and product UI microcopy.

This wasn't just marketing. It was the first usability test of the product's value proposition. 15,000+ sign-ups at 37.5% from a 40K+ organic community was the earliest product-market fit signal the team had.

LANDING TO PRODUCT

From landing page to product

The landing page and the product were designed as one system, not two separate projects.

  • The copy and framing on the landing page directly shaped the onboarding language in the product, ensuring continuity between the promise and the experience.
  • The 3-step mechanics explanation on the landing page was the basis for the in-product onboarding tooltip sequence.
  • Conversion signals from the landing (which sections had highest engagement) were used as research input for the product's information hierarchy, confirming that users cared most about the pool mechanics and on-chain resolution.
  • Waitlist users served as the first cohort for usability testing, because they had already demonstrated comprehension of the product concept, their feedback on the product was significantly more actionable than cold user recruitment would have been.
WHAT I LEARNED

Lessons Learned

In institutional contexts, conversion comes after trust, not before. Every time we pushed the CTA earlier, performance dropped. Restraint was the strategy.

Full lessons from the project

Institutional audiences respond to clarity, not flash.

Every time we added a visual element to 'make the page more exciting,' we were actually eroding the trust signal. The highest-converting version of the page was also the most restrained one. The benchmark, financial and institutional SaaS products, is deliberately austere. We learned to treat visual restraint as a deliberate product choice, not a failure of creativity.

Conversion in professional pages requires trust signals before CTAs.

The first version of the page placed the primary CTA in the hero with minimal context. Conversion rate was significantly lower. Adding a credibility and context layer before the CTA, and moving the CTA to repeat at the end of that layer, was the single highest-impact structural change. Users needed to believe before they would act.

Explaining the mechanics is a conversion feature, not a liability.

The initial hypothesis was that a long mechanics explanation would increase drop-off. The data showed the opposite: users who reached the mechanics section had higher conversion rates than those who bounced earlier. Understanding the system reduced anxiety and increased commitment. For technical products, clarity about how something works is a trust signal, not a hurdle.

The landing page is a research artifact.

The most valuable outcome of the landing page wasn't the waitlist emails, it was the behavioral data. Which sections held attention, which CTAs got clicked, which copy drove the most engagement. This data was treated as primary research input for the product design process. Building the landing page before the product was one of the best research investments the team made.

NEXT STEPS

Next Steps

A/B test headline microcopy variations

The hero headline was not tested, it was the team's best first hypothesis. With a validated conversion rate baseline, the next step is testing alternative framings of the core value proposition. Even a 5-point improvement in the headline conversion rate would compound significantly at scale.

Segment waitlist users for tailored onboarding flows

The current waitlist is unsegmented. Based on behavioral signals from the page (which sections users engaged with longest), users can be segmented into: institutional/professional (high scroll depth on mechanics sections) vs. consumer/enthusiast (high engagement with product concept sections). Each segment warrants a different onboarding sequence.

Integrate early user feedback into landing copy refinement

The first cohort of beta users, recruited through the waitlist, now has direct product experience. Their feedback on the gap between what the landing page promised and what the product delivered is the highest-quality input available for a second iteration of the page.

Add tracking to measure engagement path and drop-offs

Current data covers macro metrics (conversion rate, scroll depth). Heatmap and session recording data would identify the specific micro-drop-off points within sections, enabling surgical copy and layout improvements rather than broad structural changes.
