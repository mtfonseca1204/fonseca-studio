Problem

Hedgehog was pre-launch: no product, no screenshots, no proof. Institutional crypto users had to sign up for something they could not see yet.

Solution

A trust-first funnel that earns credibility before asking for anything. Value → trust → mechanics → signup

Result

37.5% waitlist conversion vs. 2 to 8% industry benchmark. Full impact below.

The situation

No product. No proof. Still need early adopters.

40K+ organic reach across X (14.6K), Telegram (14K), and Discord. One metric mattered: waitlist sign-up. No live product, no demo reel, no customer logos to lean on.

Every visitor arrived skeptical. Institutional and professional crypto users bounce at anything that feels premature, salesy, or unfinished. The B2B/Web3 waitlist benchmark sits at 2 to 8% conversion. Hedgehog had to beat that with $0 paid media.

How I worked

Research the trust gap, then earn each funnel step

As solo Product Designer I owned conversion architecture, copy direction, and visual language end to end. Before opening Figma, I studied how Brex, Ramp, Polymarket, and Linear earn trust before they ask for action.

What changed our direction: Institutional users need to understand the benefit before they value exclusivity. The funnel became Understand → Believe → Trust → Act. Most crypto landings skip straight to Act.

How the page fed the product: Scroll depth and section engagement shaped onboarding copy and information architecture. Waitlist sign-ups became the first real usability cohort and the earliest signal of product-market fit.

What we built

Four stages. Trust first, conversion last.

01

Hero: frame the value, don’t oversell

Clear proposition, institutional tone, no hype. CTA present but not forced. Concept visuals instead of unfinished UI mockups.

02

Trust layer: earn credibility

Problem framing that matches user reality. On-chain focus, SLA, governance, and system integrity: the questions institutional users ask.

03

Mechanics: make it believable

3-step explanation: what it is, how it works, why it’s different. Enough to believe, not enough to audit. Full docs linked for depth.

04

Conversion: close at peak intent

Repeated CTAs with evolving microcopy. Email only. Every extra field dropped conversion. Segmentation moved to onboarding.

Key choices

Three tradeoffs that drove conversion

Concept visuals over UI mockups

We considered showing product screens to prove progress. Placeholder UI signaled “not ready.” We chose concept visuals plus strong copy. Users stayed longer and converted more.

Email only in the form

We considered multi-field signup for segmentation. Each extra field dropped conversion. We chose email-only at signup and moved segmentation to onboarding.

The page as research artifact

We considered the landing as marketing only. Behavioral data (which sections held attention, where users dropped) shaped product IA and became the first usability test of the value proposition.

What changed

15,000+ sign-ups. Zero paid traffic.

37.5% conversion from organic community visitors (benchmark: 2 to 8%). 15,000+ waitlist sign-ups with $0 paid acquisition across X, Telegram, Discord, and Web3 networks.

Users who reached the mechanics section converted at significantly higher rates. High scroll retention through trust and mechanics became the earliest product-market fit signal the team had.

Looking ahead

What I’d carry forward

In institutional contexts, conversion comes after trust, not before. Every time we pushed the CTA earlier, performance dropped. Restraint was the strategy.

Next: Segment waitlist by engagement; close the promise gap with beta feedback on landing vs. product delivery.
