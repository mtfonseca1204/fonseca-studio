I wasn't brought in to polish mockups. I killed the orderbook, designed the four-state product loop, built a 120+ component system, and shipped desktop and mobile end-to-end. When users were confused, we changed the mental model first. The UI followed.

Founding product design means owning the mechanics, the loop, the system, and the proof it works.

THE PROBLEM

Non-traders couldn't enter prediction markets.

Every existing platform (Polymarket, Augur, Drift) made users understand the financial infrastructure before engaging with it. Orderbooks. Counterparties. Spread mechanics.

The result wasn't lack of interest. It was an interface that made people feel unqualified to press a button.

Light Dark

Before this screen, chaos. After it, one question: UP or DOWN?

THE SOLUTION

One decision. Short cycle. Immediate outcome.

No orderbooks. No counterparties. No liquidity knowledge required. Users choose UP or DOWN. The round closes. The result is immediate. One dominant action at all times, everything else is secondary.

01

Decision: the moment of choice

The entire interface answers one question: UP or DOWN? In v1 we showed 6 data points. We cut to 3: price, reference, and the two action buttons. Time-to-first-action dropped from 8 seconds to under 2.

02

Participation: social proof as signal

We exposed the UP/DOWN pool split publicly. Experienced users read it as a contrarian signal. New users use it as validation. Either way, it keeps people inside the product instead of searching elsewhere.

03

Waiting: transparency without anxiety

After entering a position, the user waits for resolution. Countdown timer, locked position visible, no new entries. A system state most products ignore, designed to communicate progress without creating anxiety.

04

Resolution: close the loop, trigger the next

v1 was a modal: "You won X." One tap to close. Retention was low. We replaced it with a full-screen state: result, payout, play again. Designed around the emotional peak of the cycle. Single highest-impact change for repeat engagement.

Enter → wait → resolve → repeat. Four states. One loop. That's the product.

KEY DECISIONS

Three choices that unlocked 88% completion

Killed the orderbook

We tested a simplified orderbook first. Same drop-off, same confusion, just fewer columns. The problem wasn't visual. It was the mental model. Switching to pools made the experience learnable in 30 seconds.

Made participation data public

Early versions hid the UP/DOWN split. Users left the product to find it on Twitter. We built it in. It became one of the most engaged-with features in the product.

Replaced the modal with a full screen

A modal dismisses the emotion. A full screen channels it into the next action. Immediate feedback + visual reward + direct CTA = the retention loop.

DISCOVERY & RESEARCH

We studied why prediction markets don't scale

Competitive analysis across 6 platforms (Polymarket, Augur, Drift, Binance Predictions, Azuro, Limitless) revealed the same pattern: interfaces built for financial professionals, not curious participants.

What we learned

  • Trading mechanics (orderbooks, spreads) block non-experts before the first bet
  • More data at decision time = more hesitation = more drop-off
  • Long resolution timelines kill engagement loops
  • Users naturally look at what others are doing. Hiding it sends them elsewhere
  • Short, repeatable cycles create habit. Long waits don't

One decision. Shorten the cycle. Make outcomes immediate. Expose behavior, not just data. That was the design direction.

Users, audience tension & full benchmark notes

Two user types, one interface:

Curious newcomer: interested in crypto but not a trader. Needs to understand what to do in under 30 seconds. Drops off if orientation takes longer.

Experienced DeFi user: wants fast cycles and visible pool mechanics. Disengages if the product feels too shallow.

Platform benchmark:

  • Polymarket: strong events, requires orderbook familiarity
  • Augur: powerful but high onboarding friction
  • Drift: excellent for experts, overwhelming for newcomers
  • Binance Predictions: closest fast-cycle reference we studied
  • Azuro: good UX but tied to long-resolution events
  • Limitless: interesting mechanics but unclear primary action
DESIGN SYSTEM

Clarity under pressure

Prediction involves money, time pressure, and uncertainty. The interface can't add noise on top of that.

One dominant action

UP/DOWN buttons are always the highest-contrast element on screen. Everything else supports the decision.

Numbers over decoration

The product communicates through data and states, not illustration. Every element is decision-critical or supporting context.

Consistent grid across states

Users never feel disoriented moving between decision, waiting, and resolution. Same spacing, same hierarchy.

Dark mode as default

Reinforces the real-time, high-stakes nature of the product. Reduces eye strain during extended sessions.

System scope:

  • 120+ components built in Figma across desktop and mobile
  • 4 core product states fully documented
  • Full responsive coverage at every breakpoint
  • AI-assisted workflows reduced ideation cycles by ~40%

Full metrics & delivery impact

Product performance:

Task completion: 40% (v1, orderbook) to 88% (v2, pool model) in usability sessions.

Time to first action: 8 seconds to under 2 seconds after reducing primary screen to 3 elements.

Retention signal: users who complete 3+ rounds in a session strongly correlate with return visits.

Drop-off per step: every extra step before first participation increased drop-off by ~15%.

Design & delivery:

Design system: 120+ components reduced design-to-dev handoff by ~35%.

Releases: 3 major product releases shipped across desktop and mobile.

Waitlist: 37.5% conversion, fully organic via Discord and Twitter. B2B/Web3 benchmark: 2 to 8%.

We didn't benchmark against other prediction UIs. We benchmarked against the 8 seconds it took someone to feel confident enough to press UP or DOWN.

LANDING TO PRODUCT

The waitlist became the first product test

The landing page and the product were designed as one system. The waitlist landing page converted at 37.5% with zero paid traffic. That audience became the first usability cohort.

  • Landing page copy and mechanics explanation shaped in-product onboarding tooltips.
  • Scroll depth on the landing validated which concepts users cared about most (pool mechanics, on-chain resolution).
  • Waitlist users who already understood the concept gave significantly more actionable product feedback than cold recruitment.
  • Behavioral data from the landing directly informed the four-state loop architecture.

The landing page wasn't marketing. It was the first usability test of the product loop.

WHAT I LEARNED

Lessons Learned

When users are confused, look at the mental model before touching the UI. Simplifying visually without changing the underlying logic just moves the confusion. It doesn't remove it.

Full lessons from the project

UI simplification without system simplification doesn't work.

v1 tried to reduce complexity visually while keeping the orderbook. Same drop-off patterns. The fix was pools, not prettier columns.

Showing behavior is more powerful than showing data.

The leaderboard was almost cut for feeling "gamey." It became one of the most engaged-with features. Design around actual behavior, not idealized behavior.

The resolution screen is the product's most important moment.

We spent the least time on win/loss in v1. Making it a full-screen state with a direct "Play again" CTA was the single highest-impact retention change.

Progressive disclosure needs explicit rules.

The product always loads in simple mode. Depth is accessible but never forced. Every time we violated that rule, usability metrics dropped.

NEXT STEPS

Next Steps

Onboarding for first-time participants

~30% of new users complete their first round without guidance, but only ~15% return for a second. A one-time pool mechanic explanation is the highest-leverage retention fix.

More on-chain metrics

Most requested feature from active users. Users with access to 3+ market types participate significantly more than single-market users.

Advanced analytics for experienced users

Historical performance data and round-by-round analytics. The depth layer that keeps sophisticated users engaged long-term.

Social and competitive mechanics

Users already check the leaderboard before entering. Persistent rankings and shareable outcomes would formalize what they're doing informally.
