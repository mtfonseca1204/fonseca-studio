MY ROLE

Brand Designer: translate transparency into a system the team could run

Picnic is a Brazilian fintech that eliminates hidden fees from international spending through digital dollars and blockchain technology. My job was to turn that promise into a visual language that felt approachable, fresh, and credible across every touchpoint.

I worked with the Picnic team and Head of Design Rafael Queiroz to deliver a Key Visual system built for scale: social templates, communication assets, and brand guidelines that anyone on the team could apply correctly.

Key Visual system: Flexible compositions, patterns, and graphic elements that work across formats without losing brand recognition.

Brand guidelines: Typography, color, imagery, and usage rules documented for long-term consistency.

Social templates: Instagram, LinkedIn, and campaign formats ready for the marketing team to deploy.

Communication assets: Email headers, in-app graphics, and digital banners aligned with the same visual logic.

The system had to empower the team, not depend on me for every asset. That's what we built.

THE PROBLEM

A fintech brand stuck between corporate cold and creative chaos

Picnic needed a Key Visual system that could translate their promise of transparency and savings into a cohesive visual language. The challenge was to feel approachable and fresh while maintaining the credibility expected from a fintech handling international transactions.

Without a defined system, the team faced inconsistency across social media, campaigns, and product touchpoints. Each designer or marketer interpreted the brand differently, leading to a fragmented presence that diluted brand recognition.

Breaking away from corporate blues was the starting point. Keep scrolling to see what replaced them.

THE SOLUTION

A Key Visual system built for recognition, scale, and team autonomy

The goal was a robust visual identity that empowers the Picnic team to create consistent, on-brand content with autonomy and precision. The system needed distinct recognition, scalable templates, and clear standards anyone could follow.

I built a full Key Visual system with four core deliverables:

  • Visual Identity Guidelines: Typography, color, and composition rules
  • Social Media Templates: Instagram, LinkedIn, and campaign formats
  • Communication Assets: Email headers, in-app graphics, and digital banners
  • Brand Application Examples: Reference library for the team

The result is a brand presence that feels modern, trustworthy, and distinctly Picnic, whether on Instagram, in-app, or across digital campaigns.

Templates and examples matter more than abstract rules. The team needed to see how to apply the system, not just read about it.

KEY DECISIONS

Three choices that shaped the identity

Approachable over corporate

Traditional banks lean on cold blues and stock imagery. Picnic needed to break that pattern while still earning trust for international transactions. The visual language balances warmth with fintech credibility.

Templates before abstract rules

Brand guidelines only work when teams can see them applied. Social templates, email headers, and in-app graphics became the primary teaching tool. Examples carried more weight than theory.

Built for team autonomy

Every asset was designed so marketers and designers could create on-brand content without waiting on a single person. Scalable guidelines, not one-off designs.

RESEARCH

Understanding fintech positioning before picking colors

Before designing, I focused on competitive context, existing brand materials, and alignment with the Picnic team on priorities and approval workflows.

  • Reviewing Picnic's existing brand materials, positioning docs, and competitor landscape

  • Mapping social media best practices for fintech and Web3 audiences

WHAT I LEARNED

Lessons Learned

Fintech brands don't have to look like banks. Breaking away from corporate blues and stock imagery creates distinct, memorable identities that still earn trust when the product delivers.

Full lessons from the project

Clear briefs save time.

Early alignment on "why" and "who" accelerated the visual exploration phase.

Brand guidelines must be practical.

Templates and examples matter more than abstract rules. Teams need to see how to apply the system.

Systems beat one-off designs.

Building for team autonomy from the start means the brand stays consistent long after the project ends.
